General Introduction

Welcome, to my post about oscillators. There are quite a few components in here that are not quite beginner friendly, however; I followed a schematic design and explanation by Moritz Klein. I’ve taken the liberty to expand a little on his circuit but also create a reproducible PCB board layout in Eagle. My goal with these projects is to get to a point where the majority of my modules are DIY, hoping to learn a lot about synthesizer circuits to make my own new and novel versions but also make things more accessible to those interested in both music making but also utilizing circuits as an art form.

Oscillator Introduction

The voltage controlled oscillator broken down into it’s simplest parts is a Resistor - Capacitor (RC) circuit, where a capacitor charges, raising the voltage level, and then discharges through the resistor causing the voltage level to fall. However; in order to integrate this into a eurorack format we add several other components. A mixer, an augmented oscillator core with a schmitt trigger and instead of a resistor, two transistors, and an ac-coupled op-amp. All this is built on top of a bipolar 12V.

Bipolar Power

The bipolar power is an important aspect of synthesizer electronics for two separate reasons. One, when voltage is mapped to an oscillation every voltage level is mapped to a frequency. If we only had discrete voltages between 0 volts and 5 volts, we would map that space to 128 notes. This would mean changing a note would be 0.04volts. This voltage is so fine that it is difficult to get that granularity when utilizing a transistor as a control voltage resistor. Two, operational amplifiers provide the best and easiest method to buffer and scale down our oscillation to fit within the eurorack standard.

Mixing: Component One

The first component is a parallel mixing circuit. The mixing circuit is composed of a potentiometer, a resistor and a thermistor, in series. The three components cut the 12 volt voltage level to a level low enough to make the transistor act like a resistor, which happens to transistors when a low enough voltage level hits a transistors base. The flow of current from the collector to the emitter is in a partial open and closed state, acting like a resistor.

Oscillator Core: Component Two

The second component is the oscillator core. The small voltage mixed from the potentiometers and jacks hits the base of the pnp transistor. This allows a +12V raid to sink to a -12V rail divided by a 1Mega Ohm resistor, which hits the base of NPN transistor. This triggers a voltage to fill up a capacitor and raise the voltage level beyond the schmitt triggers threshold. Next, the schmitt trigger puts out an equal but opposite signal until the voltage level falls below another threshold, which I believe are set by the schmitt triggers power raid inputs. Therefore, this cycle continues producing an oscillation.

Sawtooth Output: Component Three

The third component is a series of op-amps. The first op-amp buffers the input from the output. If this op-amp didn’t exist, then current would be dissipated with the output and detune the oscillation. Therefore, an op-amp, with its high impedance is the perfect tool to couple both the input and the output. The second op-amp for the sawtooth wave form scales the oscillation voltage down to a 10V peak to peak standard utilized in the eurorack synthesizer setup.

Square Output: Component Four

For the square wave, the schematic scales the threshold of the sawtooth wave and send it into the schmitt trigger. Since the schmitt trigger only turns on and off above and below a particular threshold, the amount of time the oscillation is above and below those thresholds will create the duty cycle, or rather the amount of time that the square wave is high relative to how often the square wave is low. Hence, we have a potentiometer on the op-amps inverted rail. After the schmitt trigger we scale the oscillation to be sent to another eurorack module or an output.

Version 3 and Beyond

My latest build of this oscillator, V2, contains 8 out of 9 inputs and outputs working, with the only missing output, being a square wave on channel 2. I found this to be a success but going into the future, I would like to add an LED to illustrate that the module is getting power, a triangle wave form on a third board underneath the logic board for the other two oscillators, flip the trimmers around for easier tuning, and fix an issue where the square wave cuts the oscillation when the duty cycle is high enough. However; my next project is most likely going to be a triple VCA to give the oscillations some life.
