High Museum of Art

High museum was one of the largest LED installations Charles had ever engineered. There was more than three-hundred meters of LED tape spread between multiple rooms that were integrated with encoder controllers. The challenges were to figure out how to spread a pwm signal over long distance without the signal attentuating, how to inject power to avoid voltage drop, keep the light emitting diodes from overheating, and designing easy access points for technicians maintaining the electrical components. In order to pull this off, Charle's first began with the design drawings to ensure easy access to the leds and that ventilation was appropriated in the designs. Next, calculations were done to ensure power drops were placed every five meters and dmx to pwm controllers were placed close to the leds to avoid the attenuation of the pwm signal. Afterwards, there were countless hours integrated, and soldering the leds into the exhibit cabinetry. As a result, the space was flooded with leds that contained intuitive control interfaces from rotation knobs to sliding potentiometers. It offered a robust design for children of all ages to interact and explore the realm of art and color.

CR-Smith Museum

Charles's first professional engineering experiences started with CRsmith, an American Airlines sponsered museum, about flight. Charles took on the task to design and develop the center-peice of the exhibit space, an authentic MD80 cockpit. The peice came straight out of a junkyard and was shipped to the shop in Columbus, Ohio. In the midst of the complexity of the cockpit, parts needed to be torn out and replaced to become a dynamic display case. Therefore, projects, buttons, leds, and speakers all had to make a home in the cockpit, that were easy to access but also couldn't be tampered by children or nosy adults. First, Charles had to collaborate with mechanical engineers and project managers to determine the best position for all the components. Next Charles had to roll up his sleeves and begin pulling a lot of the natural wiring out of the cockpit in order to run wires for power and data to the exhibit. Afterwards the parts were integrated into cockpit and tested a week before it shipped. The cockpit now exist in Fort Worth, Texas, and is a big hit with visiting pilots as well as American Airlines staff. It is as close as one can get to piloting a real MD80 without being a pilot themselves.

Discovery World Energy

Discovery World was easily the most challenging programming task Charles ever accomplished. Discover World, a science museum in Milwaukee, were looking to add an exhibit about energy and power. Charles's task involved programming a complex system that contained 3 microcontrollers, 3 gear tooth sensors, 7 alternating current devices, 24 seven-segment displays, and over 300 leds. The system had to run concurrently, be tested and developed in under two weeks. First course of action was to begin programming immediately. He first split the microcontrollers into three different modules that depending on partiular pins being wired to high and low signals loading different objects in the code and were responsible for different tasks. This allowed Charles to split the number of states in actions into separate parts and made the system more manageable. Next each state was separately coded and tested. First, the gear tooth sensors and the data structure that incremented how fast someone was pedaling. Next, the led animation then the 7 segment displays (which were brand new and needed an api to be tested and written) and lastly the relays controlling the AC devices. Then it was time to put all the parts together and see how they interacted with one-another. Fortunately with such a limited amount of time all the tests and features passed, but with a stress on optimization and memory management. In the field, however; the exhibit still stands and was one of the most resilient in the space.
