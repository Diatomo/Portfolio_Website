Otherworld

Otherworld was a small company that designed and built interactive art spaces. They built rooms and hallways filled with escape room and haunted house elements. This included enigmatic puzzles, fantastical sculpture, audio, and textiles as well as interactive projection mapping and otherworldy led animations. Charles was hired on for a technologist position to build interactive LED systems and develop a narrative display using the Unity Framework. The size and scope of the projects produced some challenges that required tenacity as well as innovative problem solving.

Infinity Hall

The Infinity Hall was a built hallway with LED channels that drew the attention to altered perspectives within the reflections of the mirrors but also the length of the hallway itself. The exhibit played with one's perspective in two different ways. First, the hallway narrows, so when one looks down the hallway it appears much further away than it really was. Second, the narrowing caused the mirrors' reflections to curve. Giving the sense that one was standing in a circular room. These perspectives were then accentuated by the led channels placed in the walls, ceiling, and floor. With a chase animation that drew individuals into the alternate reality. The challenges of this exhibit were to mount the leds to a static structure and also route the wires to a central location about fifty feet from the exhibit space. It involved weeks of testing, soldering, and adhering the leds to the exhibit. It was meticulous work but eventually each led strand was mapped and animated using a teensy modification with touch designer.

Sleeper Pods

The Sleeper Pods were another large scale LED exhibit with some futuristic fabrication and the use of audio and induction sensors. There were three pods with pressure pads in the bottom. When three people were simultaneously standing on these pressure pads, there was a payoff animation. Led animations would chase to the top of the room as if people are being beamed into some kind of celestial orb going to another world. The main challenge of this exhibit was that the leds were all mounted to the ceiling which proved to be cumbersome to solder upside-down everyday. Another issue was that the leds would flicker. What Charles found out through hours of testing was that because we were using multiple microcontrollers on the same LED power circuit, the differences in data grounds caused the leds to flicker. Therefore, when the grounds were all bridged all the flicker would be resolved. What resulted was another other worldy exhibit that transformed guest's experience from everyday reality.

Church Scene

The church scene offered a few challenges. First the exhibit consisted of a rear projector, a sodium street light, 4 LED spotlights, 4 fans, an audio player, a magnet sensor, as well as 4 plants that would trigger when a door to a casket was opened and closed. The first challenge was to get looping audio and video to play. Charles first thought a raspberry pi was a great solution. However; there were a few problems with this decision. First, to safely exhibitize a raspberry pi it needs to be placed in read only mode. This, however; causes the graphics driver on the raspberry pi to become compromised and so video can no longer play. Therefore, Charles utilized a looping video player called a sprite to resolve that issue. Next, the raspberry pi, when in a box with a projector, easily overheats. No matter the heatsinks nor the ventilation it didn't seem to behave properly with the conditions. As a result, the raspberry pi was swapped for a wavtrigger which was triggered by an arduino uno. When all the issues were resolved an exhibit emerged that played heavily with light, color, and emerging audio. The sodium lamp would suck all the color from the room and when the casket was opened led spot lights were shine on beautiful painted murals and the color would seemlessly pour into a once monochromatic room.

Unity Display

Charles had the oppurtunity to develop a narrative display case with Unity. He didn't have much time but in about a week he learned a new framework and designed a mock desktop to display media content such as video, images, text, and audio. The display explained the background of Otherworld Industries. That is, a science laboratory gone wrong that mixes together life and death, space, time, and sketchy monstrous experiments that invoke one's worst childhood nightmares. The programming for the application utilized C# and was relatively straight forward. Unity utilized a collection of objects that were composited to create more complex aesthetics and interactions between objects. Therefore, Charles utilized a factory method that depending on the tag of each object would mitigate the appropriate responses. Therefore, the complexity of the code resided in a few if-then statements which routed the control of individual objects. As a result, guests can view and read the narrative of the space and learn a little more about Otherworld Industries.
